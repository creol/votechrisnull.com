Strong cities. Limited county. Lower taxes.

As Cities Grow, County Government Must Shrink

Consider this: Salt Lake County government is one of the largest employers in the county.

Top 4 Employers in Salt Lake County:

  1. Intermountain Health — 20,000+ employees
  2. University of Utah — 20,000+ employees
  3. Granite School District — 7,000–9,000 employees
  4. Salt Lake County Government — 7,000+ employees

Think about that. A county government is competing with a world-class hospital system and a major university for the title of largest employer. Intermountain saves lives. The University of Utah educates the next generation and drives innovation. What is county government doing with a workforce that large? Running parks and rec centers that your city already runs. Operating health clinics that pharmacies and private providers already cover. Building trails and pools while proposing the largest property tax increase in decades.

Government should not be one of the biggest employers in any county. Government should be lean, focused, and limited to what only government can do.

You're Paying Twice

Right now, if you live in a city like Riverton, Bluffdale, Herriman, or South Jordan, you're paying twice for the same services. Your city tax bill funds parks, recreation, youth programs, and public safety — and then your county tax bill charges you again for the exact same things.

That's double taxation, and it needs to stop.

The truth is simple: the government closest to your door is more effective, more responsive, and more aligned with your community's needs. Your city knows your neighborhood. Your city plans your growth. Your city should deliver your services.

It's Already Been Proven

When Riverton took over services like policing and animal control from the county, taxpayers saved over $20 million, gained 14 additional police officers, saw faster response times — and property taxes went down. Better service, lower cost, full accountability.

Utah law doesn't require the county to keep running parks, rec centers, or health clinics in cities that can handle these services themselves. These are discretionary programs the county chooses to duplicate — and you pay for it.

Meanwhile, the county's budget has ballooned to $2.48 billion, and leadership proposed the largest property tax increase in decades. That's not fiscal responsibility — that's a county government that refuses to right-size itself as our cities have grown.

My Plan Is Straightforward

Work with cities to transition duplicate services to local control, where they belong. As cities take on more, the county takes on less — and your property taxes go down. The county should focus on what only the county can do: courts, the sheriff, jails, roads, and regional infrastructure.

Strong cities. Limited county. Lower taxes.
